Quantum Computing Just Made Real Money (Maybe): HSBC & IBM's Bond Trading Experiment

Oct 4, 2025 ~10 min read

HSBC and IBM just dropped what they're calling "the world's first known empirical evidence" that quantum computers can actually solve real-world problems in finance.12 The headline number? A 34% improvement in prediction accuracy compared to classical techniques.

Now, reading this headline, I had so many questions. What underlying feature of this setup actually contributed to this claimed 34% improvement? Was it as simple as quantum = better? If so, why didn't they use a purely quantum approach? What makes this particular bond market prediction problem suitable for quantum computers to tackle?

Let's dig in.

What Were They Actually Trying to Do?

Trading divisions at banks like HSBC and UBS respond to client requests for them to quote prices on financial products, including bonds. Winning these trades is partly a prediction game—traders and quants use algorithms to estimate how likely their quoted price will beat competitors' in the market.

The specific problem HSBC tackled was predicting the "fill probability"—basically, what's the chance that a given bond quote would actually execute? The algorithm has to factor in a myriad of variables (market conditions, bond liquidity, risk metrics, etc.), making it a highly dimensional prediction problem.

Here's the workflow they used:

  1. Quantum transformation: This experiment used a quantum algorithm to transform/analyze the noisy market data in a way that highlights hidden predictive signals that classical algorithms might miss. The output of IBM's Heron quantum processor—essentially new features extracted from the data—was then fed into standard machine-learning models (probably something like logistic regression or SVMs).2,3
  2. Classical prediction: Those quantum-derived features got fed into regular ML models, which outputted the "fill probability" of a given bond quote.
  3. Backtesting: Testing these probabilities on historical European corporate bond trades showed that the models augmented with these quantum-derived features scored 34% better than those using purely classical approaches.1,2

But here's where it gets interesting—and weird.

The Tech Bit: Why Quantum?

Quantum computers operate fundamentally differently from the computers we use today. Classical computers use bits that are either 0 or 1 at any given time. Quantum computers use qubits (quantum bits) that can exist as 0, 1, or both 0 and 1 simultaneously. This property allows a system of n qubits to represent 2^n states at once, creating a vastly larger computational space to work with.

Here's why this matters: many patterns in our real world don't occur in such a way that classical computers can model them naturally with their 0s and 1s, so they estimate them to get a "good enough" model. Quantum computers, due to having this third possible state, can naturally model certain patterns that classical computers struggle with.

For HSBC's bond trading problem, quantum algorithms could identify non-linear patterns and correlations in market data that classical feature engineering might miss—or would need massive computational resources to approximate.

But why not go fully quantum?

IBM's Heron processor—like all current quantum computers—is inherently noisy. This noise comes from three main sources:

  1. Environmental decoherence: Qubits are stupidly sensitive to any environmental interference (electromagnetic radiation, tiny temperature changes), causing quantum states to decay
  2. Gate errors: The control pulses aren't perfect, so quantum operations don't execute exactly as intended
  3. Measurement errors: Reading qubit states introduces additional uncertainty

This noise means quantum calculations accumulate errors, which kind of introduces the need for classical computers to clean everything up before it's outputted as probabilities. Today's quantum computers can't handle end-to-end complex workflows—hence the hybrid approach.

Here's the weird part: this might have actually helped generalize the model—acting as a source of beneficial randomness that classical algorithms can't easily replicate.3 In machine learning, techniques like dropout or noise injection can prevent overfitting. The quantum hardware noise might have acted similarly. It really warrants further investigation, because this would mean today's imperfect, non-error-corrected quantum computers might be useful noise signal generators to use alongside classical AI, before full error-free quantum is scientifically possible.

This 34% improvement wasn't actually replicated when researchers tried simulating the quantum processor on a classical computer. This could imply that there's something about a real quantum computer, with its imperfections, that added value beyond what today's classical computers could replicate.

Why This Matters (Maybe)

Ok, it's pretty cool, and 34% sounds very impressive—especially if you think about how much revenue that adds for a bank if you apply such an algorithm across trading desks and it performs with that kind of improvement in accuracy.

For HSBC, this represents validation of a six-year quantum research program that includes partnerships with Quantinuum and IBM.1 They've been exploring this space since 2019, and with that investment, they obviously believe that as quantum hardware continues to evolve, their competitive edge will "continue to grow." The bet is on being a first-mover paying off.

For IBM, it's validation that their "quantum-centric supercomputing strategy" is the right one—integrating quantum processors with classical systems to solve real-world problems. Delivering practical value is central to IBM's quantum program, which already has more than $1 billion in contracts from industry and research, including JPMorgan and Wells Fargo in finance, as well as airlines and automakers.2 IBM's roadmap has aimed to demonstrate practical quantum advantage by 2026, and this collaboration does just that—albeit with a few caveats.

But Let's Pump the Brakes

The truth is, quantum computing is still very early stage. This was a successful experiment, but it was much more a research proof-of-concept than something HSBC can just deploy for use today.

The noise paradox: The most striking thing? The HSBC-IBM paper itself acknowledges that the quantum advantage disappeared when the quantum algorithm was run without hardware noise on a simulator.3,5 Computer scientist Scott Aaronson jumped on this, noting: "They see a quantum advantage… but only because of the noise in their quantum hardware. When they simulate the noiseless quantum computation, the advantage disappears."5

Simply put, a quirk of the current technology rather than a fundamental leap in computing power may have helped the results. Aaronson argues this is "an admission that the 'advantage' is just a strange artifact…nothing to do with quantum mechanics or quantum speedup." He warns that we shouldn't declare true quantum supremacy for finance based on such results, as the gain might not generalize or persist once quantum hardware improves (and the "noise" disappears). Which would be ironic.

Other red flags to consider:

  • This was one experiment, on one dataset, in one market (European corporate bonds). Does it generalize to other markets or time periods? Unknown.
  • What were the "classical techniques" they compared against? Were they testing against cutting-edge deep learning approaches, or just basic models?
  • The experiment ran on specialized hardware in IBM's labs and on their cloud—not from the trading desk itself. IBM's quantum chips have to be kept at ultra-cold temperatures near absolute zero (−273.135°C)—an environment you can't expect in a bank's server room or data centers today.6
  • There's no evidence the results are repeatable, scalable, or cost-effective. Quantum compute time is expensive.
  • This was backtesting on historical data, not live trading. Backtests often overstate real-world performance.

So What Does This Actually Mean?

This doesn't mean the HSBC result is worthless—but it does mean we should be cautious about framing it as proof that quantum computers have definitively outclassed classical algorithms in finance.

Nevertheless, the HSBC-IBM quantum trial is a milestone—showing that even today's imperfect quantum computers could possibly yield improved outcomes to real-world problems. It's a piece of validation for companies investing in quantum research.

As one commentator puts it, this is less a "Sputnik moment" and more like a "starting gun"6—signaling that the race toward practical quantum applications has begun. We're still in the early laps, but major financial institutions are moving from quantum research to quantum experimentation with production-adjacent problems. That shift—from "maybe someday" to "testing now"—is the real signal.

The advantage may be temporary, artifact-driven, and expensive—but it's no longer purely theoretical. And that's interesting.
