This website was vibe coded

Nov 23, 2024 ~5 min read

Vibe coding is everywhere…but I still think it's underrated.

I've always been drawn to things that feel deliberately designed—websites that get you hooked to the content, museum routes that flow seamlessly through history, even the king of pens that is the biro. One website that stuck with me is lovefrom.com (Jony Ive's design firm). It has virtually no content, and yet.

For years I had a picture in my head of what my own site should feel like. I tried different versions, rebuilt them, tweaked them, but the final result never fully matched the idea. Not wrong, just not right.

Then I downloaded Cursor and tried again. I let the design guide the build, instead letting my imagination being consumed by a pre-built template. The freedom in that approach with the guardrails of a rulebook I followed from Reddit, finally produced something that resembled the vision.

At first, vibe coding feels like you're cheating. Soon enough though, as you add more, you realise some early generations hid little landmines deep in the code. Fixing them means tracing decisions the model made long before you noticed anything was off. Classic 80/20: most of it comes together quickly, and the stubborn parts take the longest.

Quick tips for anyone wanting to vibe code:

  • Use Cursor - easy to use, always has the latest models (inc. Gemini 3, Opus 4.5)
  • Reddit has some mind-blowing advice, as usual - here is the guide I religiously stuck to
  • Vibe coding ≠ outsourcing, it should be treated as a collaboration tool. Let the model accelerate you, but stay close enough to the build that you can catch the weirdness before it spreads
